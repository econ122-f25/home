Introduction

In this activity, youll use K-means clustering to group colleges based on their characteristics. Unlike supervised learning where we have labeled outcomes, clustering is an unsupervised learning method that finds natural groupings in data.

Part 6: Reflection Questions

Q17: Would you expect clustering results to be identical if you ran the algorithm with a different random seed? Why or why not?

Your answer:

No, the clustering results would likely NOT be identical with a different random seed, but they should be very similar.

Heres why: - K-means starts by randomly placing K initial cluster centers (centroids) - The algorithm then iteratively assigns points to the nearest centroid and recalculates centroids - Different initial placements (from different random seeds) can lead to different local optima - K-means is guaranteed to converge, but not necessarily to the global optimum

However, we used nstart=20, which means the algorithm runs 20 times with different random starting points and returns the best result (lowest total within-cluster sum of squares). This makes the results much more stable across different seeds - while individual runs might vary, the best result among 20 runs is likely to be similar regardless of the overall random seed.

In practice with our data, the main clusters (elite selective schools, moderately selective schools, etc.) should be very similar across different seeds, though individual colleges on the boundaries between clusters might occasionally be assigned differently. The higher the nstart value, the more consistent the results will be across different random seeds.

Q18: Why is it important to standardize variables before performing K-means clustering? What did you observe when comparing your clustering results before and after scaling (Q5 vs.Q10)? Even with scaling, what issue remained (Q11)?

Your answer:

Standardization is crucial for K-means clustering because:

  1. K-means uses Euclidean distance: The algorithm assigns points to clusters based on their distance to cluster centers. Without standardization, variables with larger scales dominate the distance calculation.

  2. All variables should contribute equally (mathematically): Variables measured in larger units (like FullTime: 0-30,000+, AvgSalary: $15,000-$50,000, or cost: $0-$50,000) will have much more influence than variables measured in smaller units (like adm_rate: 0-1 or Donate: 0-1).

What we observed: - Before scaling (Q5): Variables with large scales (FullTime, cost, AvgSalary) completely dominated the clustering. The clusters didnt make clear sense based on selectivity measures alone (SAT scores and admission rates).

  • After scaling (Q10): Scaling gave all eight variables equal mathematical weight. The clusters improved significantly and we could see clearer patterns based on combinations of selectivity, cost, size, and outcomes.

  • The remaining issue (Q11): Even with perfect scaling, our variables measure different dimensions - selectivity (adm_rate, avg_sat, HStop10), financial (cost), size (FullTime), outcomes (AvgSalary), and engagement (Donate, Ratio). These dimensions dont all correlate with each other, so clusters represent multidimensional institutional profiles rather than pure selectivity groupings.

Key lesson: Scaling solves the numerical scale problem, but doesnt solve the conceptual issue of including variables that measure different dimensions of college characteristics. For purely selectivity-based clusters, we should use only selectivity variables, or accept that our clusters represent combinations of multiple institutional characteristics including size, cost, and outcomes.

Q19: Name one advantage and one disadvantage of K-means clustering compared to supervised learning methods (like decision trees or random forests).

Your answer:

Advantage of K-means clustering: K-means doesnt require labeled training data. In many real-world situations, we dont have pre-defined categories or outcomes for our data. For example, with our college data, we didnt need someone to first categorize colleges as elite, good, or average - the algorithm discovered natural groupings in the data on its own. This makes clustering useful for exploratory data analysis, discovering hidden patterns, or when labeling data would be expensive or subjective.

Disadvantage of K-means clustering: K-means requires you to specify the number of clusters (K) in advance, and the results can be harder to interpret or validate since theres no ground truth to compare against. With supervised learning, we have actual outcomes to predict and can measure accuracy, but with K-means, determining whether our clusters are good or meaningful is more subjective. Additionally, K-means assumes spherical clusters and can perform poorly when the natural groups in the data have irregular shapes, whereas some supervised methods (like random forests) can capture more complex patterns.

Additional consideration: K-means finds groupings based on similarity, while supervised learning makes predictions. They serve different purposes - clustering for exploration and pattern discovery, supervised learning for prediction and classification.
