What is Feature Engineering?

  • The process of creating new, more predictive features from raw data.
  • It uses domain knowledge to improve a simple predictive analysis.
  • Well use a hypothetical e-commerce customer dataset as an example.

Our Goal

  • Understand the relationships between our variables and a customers total_spend.
  • Use feature engineering techniques to transform existing variables and create new ones.
  • Analyze the correlation of these features with total_spend to find stronger relationships.

Creating a New Feature: Days Since Purchase

Well start by creating a new feature: days_since_purchase. This is a more meaningful measure of a customers engagement than the raw purchase_date.

# Feature Engineering: Calculate days since last purchase
customer_data <- customer_data %>%
  mutate(
    days_since_purchase = as.numeric(today - purchase_date)
  )

print(head(customer_data %>% select(customer_id, purchase_date, days_since_purchase)))
## # A tibble: 6  3
##   customer_id purchase_date days_since_purchase

  • Weve transformed a date variable into a numeric variable that is easier for models to interpret. A larger number of days_since_purchase suggests a less recent customer interaction.

Correlation Analysis: New Feature

Lets see if the correlation with total_spend is stronger for the new days_since_purchase feature than for the original age variable.

## Correlation of total_spend and age: -0.02692026
## Correlation of total_spend and days_since_purchase: -0.07640746
  • The new feature, days_since_purchase, shows a stronger correlation with total_spend than age
  • This indicates that as the days since a customers last purchase increase, their total spend tends to decrease, which is a powerful insight.
  • Age alone remains a weak predictor, but there may be more complex relationships at play.

Handling Categorical Data (One-Hot Encoding)

  • Most correlation analyses require numerical data
  • We want to know if the product categories are correlated with total spend
  • We need to convert the string variable into variables for each category
  • These are called binary indicator or dummy variables
# Load the fastDummies package for one-hot encoding
library(fastDummies)
# Feature Engineering: One-hot encode the product_category
encoded_customer_data <- customer_data %>% dummy_cols(select_columns = "product_category")
# Print output
print(head(encoded_customer_data %>% select(product_category, starts_with("product_category_"))))
## # A tibble: 6  4

Creating a New Feature: Interaction Term

We can create a new feature by multiplying two existing features, such as age and days_since_purchase. This can capture a more complex relationship where the effect of one variable depends on the other.

# Feature Engineering: Create an interaction term
customer_data <- customer_data %>% 
  mutate(age_x_days_since_purchase = age * days_since_purchase)

print(head(customer_data %>% select(customer_id, age, days_since_purchase, age_x_days_since_purchase)))
## # A tibble: 6  4
##   customer_id   age days_since_purchase age_x_days_since_purchase
##         <int> <dbl>               <dbl>                     <dbl>
## 1           1    33                 130                      4290
## 2           2    59                 134                      7906
## 3           3    39                 106                      4134
## 4           4    64                  30                      1920
## 5           5    67                 134                      8978
## 6           6    20                  66                      1320
  • This new interaction term can help a model understand if the effect of age on spending changes depending on how long its been since a purchase, or vice versa.

Correlation Analysis: With Interaction Term

Now lets see how the new interaction term correlates with total_spend.

## Correlation of total_spend and age: -0.02692026
## Correlation of total_spend and days_since_purchase: -0.07640746
## Correlation of total_spend and age_x_days_since_purchase: 0.1562378
  • The correlation of the interaction term is much stronger than either individual variable alone, demonstrating a powerful interaction effect.
  • This shows that while age and days since purchase individually are weak predictors, their combination reveals a strong relationship with total spend.

The Iterative Process & Conclusion

  • Feature engineering is an iterative process of generating ideas, creating new features, evaluating their impact, and refining them.
  • The goal is to find stronger relationships and better-prepared data for predictive models.
  • In our example, the interaction between age and days since purchase revealed a much stronger relationship than either variable alone.
  • This process transforms a simple dataset into a rich feature set.
  • Knowing which features to generate requires domain knowledge and may be one task that AI is not yet good at
